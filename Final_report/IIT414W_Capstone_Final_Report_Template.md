## §1 — Executive Summary

This tool helps a race strategy engineer decide, on the Saturday night before a Grand Prix, whether to plan one pit stop or two — and which tyre compounds to use in each sequence. It does not make the decision for the team. Instead, it produces a probability estimate for two different race objectives — finishing in the top ten, and finishing on the podium — and shows how those estimates change when the planned strategy changes. The key output is whether the two objectives agree on the same strategy recommendation or conflict with each other.

The headline finding from this project is that they conflict, and that the conflict matters. At the Hungarian Grand Prix 2023, with Sergio Pérez starting from ninth place in a Red Bull, the model evaluated a one-stop conservative strategy (Medium compound, then Soft) against a two-stop aggressive strategy (Medium, then Hard, then Hard). The model predicted that the two-stop plan raised the probability of finishing in the top ten from 80.0% to 91.4%, a gain of 11.36 percentage points. At the same time, it predicted that the two-stop plan reduced the probability of a podium finish from 25.0% to 2.1%, a loss of 22.87 percentage points. A single-target tool that only tracks top-ten probability would have recommended the two-stop plan enthusiastically, without ever displaying the podium cost. A team whose race objective was to fight for a podium — which is credible for Red Bull at Hungaroring from ninth on the grid — would have followed that recommendation and lost the podium window without ever knowing it existed. The dual-target design prevents this failure by making the trade-off visible, not by resolving it.

There is one limit the team must understand before acting on any podium probability estimate from this tool. The model that produces those estimates, trained to predict whether a driver will finish in the top three, does not outperform a far simpler model that uses only the driver's starting grid position. On the test set covering the 2023 and 2024 seasons, the seven-feature model returns a Brier score of 0.0805 while the grid-position-only model returns 0.0741. A lower Brier score means better probability estimates, so the simple baseline wins on this measure. This tells us that the podium probability estimates are primarily shaped by where the car starts on the grid — in other words, by the car's underlying pace — rather than by the strategy choice itself. The direction of the disagreement between the two strategies is real and robust. The absolute numbers, 25.0% versus 2.1%, should be treated as approximate bounds on the trade-off, not as precise forecasts of what will happen race day.

The recommendation is straightforward: always consult both targets together. When they agree, the recommendation is clear. When they disagree, as in the Pérez scenario, the team must decide whether its objective for that race is points or podium, and then choose accordingly. Using the top-ten model alone is appropriate only when podium is not a realistic outcome for the car in question — which is the case for backmarker constructors but not for front-tier teams like Red Bull.

## §2 — Problem Framing

The primary user of this tool is the race strategy engineer at an F1 constructor, working during the pre-race preparation window that opens on Saturday evening after qualifying and closes at the formation lap on Sunday morning. This is when the strategy desk commits to the planned number of pit stops, the intended tyre compound sequence, and the approximate pit-window laps. Once the race begins, adjustments are made in real time as safety cars, incidents, and weather change the picture, but the plan locked in before the formation lap sets the default trajectory for the race. The question this tool addresses is: given everything we know about this driver, this car, and this circuit before the race starts, which of two candidate strategies gives us the better expected outcome?

The decision has two dimensions that are not interchangeable. The first is whether the car finishes inside the top ten, which determines whether it scores championship points at all. The second is whether the car finishes on the podium — inside the top three — which determines whether it contends for the race win and delivers the kind of result that matters for a team chasing a constructor championship or a driver chasing a personal milestone. A strategy that maximizes the probability of scoring points may simultaneously minimize the probability of a podium. The two objectives pull in opposite directions when the car is in a genuinely competitive position — for example, starting from mid-grid with a front-tier constructor. A tool that tracks only one of them will silently optimize for one objective while ignoring the cost to the other.

The model is trained to answer a specific, bounded question: under the historical relationship between pre-race conditions and race outcomes observed in the 2019–2021 Formula 1 seasons, and holding fixed all context about the driver, constructor, and circuit, how does the probability of finishing in the top ten or top three change when we vary only the number of pit stops and the tyre compound sequence? The time budget for this question is the pre-race window, which means all inputs must be observable before the formation lap. The model does not receive any data from the race itself — no live timing, no safety car status, no evolving tyre degradation estimates.

Three assumptions underlie this framing and each carries a consequence the team must be aware of.

The first assumption is that tyre selection communicated to the FIA before the race partially reveals the team's intended strategy. In practice, teams declare their tyre set selection at the event, which constrains the compound sequence they can run. The model uses the observed pit stop count and compound sequence from the historical data, treating these as the strategy that was actually executed. This means the model learns the correlation between executed strategies and race outcomes, not the causal effect of one strategy choice over another. Teams that chose a one-stop conservative strategy in the training data may have done so because their car had the pace to defend track position — so the correlation between one-stop and podium outcomes at tyre-preservation circuits partly reflects car quality, not strategy superiority.

The second assumption is that constructor tier is stable within a season. The model captures team pace through the `constructor_tier` categorical feature and through rolling form averages over the prior three races. If a constructor's competitiveness changes significantly mid-season — through a significant upgrade, a regulation clarification, or a strategic refocus — the model's pace representation becomes stale. This is particularly relevant for the 2022 regulation reset, when some teams that were classified as midfield in the training period improved dramatically.

The third assumption is that the 2019–2021 training window is representative enough of 2023–2024 conditions for the model's learned relationships to transfer. This window coincides with the Mercedes and Red Bull dual-dominance era and the pre-2022 aerodynamic regulations. The 2022 regulation change substantially reshuffled the competitive order, and the 2023–2024 seasons saw Red Bull enter a period of unprecedented single-team dominance. A model trained on 2019–2021 patterns may underestimate the degree to which car performance, rather than strategy choice, determines outcomes in the test period.

Two metrics are used to evaluate the model: Brier score and ROC-AUC. Brier score measures calibration quality — whether the model's stated probability of, say, 25% for a podium is actually observed about 25% of the time in the data. Good calibration is essential for a strategy advisor because the probability estimates are the core output. If a model says 25% but is actually right only 10% of the time, the team cannot use the number in a rational decision calculation. ROC-AUC measures ranking quality — whether the model assigns higher probabilities to drivers who do finish in the top three than to drivers who do not, regardless of whether the probability values themselves are well-calibrated. Both metrics are necessary: ROC-AUC without calibration means the model can rank drivers correctly but its probability values cannot be trusted as inputs to a trade-off calculation. Brier score without ROC-AUC means the model is well-calibrated on average but may not distinguish between good and poor strategic options in a specific scenario.

## §3 — Data and Validation

The dataset is `f1_strategy_race_level.csv`, provided as part of the IIT414W course materials for the 2026 term. It contains 2,447 rows and 47 columns. Each row represents one driver's performance in one race, making the unit of observation a driver-race. The dataset covers the 2019 through 2024 Formula 1 seasons, spanning six seasons of the World Championship. The 47 columns include pre-race context features such as grid position and rolling form averages, strategy variables recorded post-race such as the number of pit stops and tyre compound sequences, race-condition audit variables such as safety car laps and weather observations, and outcome variables including finish position, points scored, and the binary top-ten and top-three indicators used as the model's targets.

The temporal split is course-mandated and locked. The training set covers the 2019, 2020, and 2021 seasons and contains 1,132 rows. The calibration set covers the 2022 season and contains 426 rows. The test set covers the 2023 and 2024 seasons and contains 889 rows. The split is temporal rather than random because a random split would allow future race outcomes to influence the training data. If the model were trained on a randomly selected 80% of all rows spanning 2019 to 2024, it could learn patterns from 2023 races and then be evaluated on other 2023 races — effectively fitting to future information. The temporal split ensures that the model never sees data from a season it is evaluated on, making the test-set metrics a genuine out-of-sample estimate of how the model would perform if deployed at the start of the 2023 or 2024 season.

On the test set, the primary target `is_top10` has a positive-class rate of 0.517, meaning 51.7% of driver-races in the test period ended in a top-ten finish. This is near-balanced and reflects the structure of F1 competition, where ten of the twenty drivers on the grid finish in the points by definition. The expansion target `is_top3` has a positive-class rate of 0.155, meaning 15.5% of driver-races ended in a podium finish. This is meaningfully imbalanced — roughly one in six races — and this asymmetry has direct consequences for model behavior, as detailed in §4.

The leakage audit classifies every column in the dataset into one of five categories before any model is trained. The `pre_race` class contains features that are genuinely observable before the race starts, such as `grid_position`, rolling form averages, `constructor_tier`, and `circuit_type`. The `scenario_input` class contains strategy variables that are observed post-race in the raw data but declared as user-controlled inputs for the what-if comparison; these include `n_stops`, `compound_sequence`, and all individual stint length fields. The `audit` class contains race-condition variables observed during or after the race that are useful for slicing error analysis but cannot be used as features; these include safety car statistics, weather observations, and temperature readings. The `outcome` class contains the model's targets and other post-race results. The `id` class contains identifier columns that are index or filter keys only and carry no predictive signal.

During the audit, five columns were found to be initially unclassified: `circuit_id`, `constructor_name`, `driver_name`, `race_name`, and `stint_lengths`. After inspection, the four name and identifier columns — `circuit_id`, `constructor_name`, `driver_name`, and `race_name` — were classified as `id`, as each is redundant with an already-classified identifier already present in the dataset (`circuit`, `Team`, `Driver`, and `circuit` respectively). The `stint_lengths` column was classified as `audit`, as it is a derived concatenation of the individual `stint1_length` through `stint5_length` fields, which are themselves post-race observations.

Three columns were removed from the feature set relative to an earlier Hito 1 analysis. `qualifying_position` was removed because it is collinear with `grid_position` — both encode the same pre-race ranking information, and including both would introduce multicollinearity without adding predictive value. `avg_track_temp` and `avg_air_temp` were removed because they are post-race observations, not pre-race forecasts. Including them would constitute data leakage: a model trained on post-race temperature data would have access to information that cannot be known before the formation lap.

The scenario protocol for the what-if comparison is strict. Only two features — `n_stops` and `compound_sequence` — are varied between the two candidate strategies. All other feature values are held at the values recorded in the actual historical race row for the driver and race in question. This satisfies the leakage policy because `n_stops` and `compound_sequence` are the only `scenario_input` columns included in the model's feature set. Varying any other column would either modify a pre-race context feature (which would change the driver-race identity, not the strategy) or introduce a post-race observation (which is not available before the formation lap).

## §4 — Modeling Approach

Both the primary model for `is_top10` and the expansion model for `is_top3` use an identical seven-feature set. This is a deliberate design constraint. Five of the features are pre-race context variables: `grid_position` captures the driver's starting position on the grid, determined by qualifying results; `driver_prior3_avg_finish` captures the driver's average finishing position over the three most recent races, providing a measure of current form; `constructor_prior3_avg_finish` captures the constructor's corresponding three-race average, reflecting team pace; `driver_circuit_prior_avg` captures the driver's historical average finish at the specific circuit, encoding track-specific adaptation; and `constructor_tier` is a categorical variable that classifies the team as front, midfield, or backmarker based on its typical competitive level within the season. The two remaining features are scenario inputs: `n_stops` records the number of planned pit stops, and `compound_sequence` records the ordered sequence of tyre compounds used across the race stints.

The form-based features — `driver_prior3_avg_finish`, `constructor_prior3_avg_finish`, and `driver_circuit_prior_avg` — were chosen because they capture pace without requiring real-time telemetry, which is not available before the race. `grid_position` is the single strongest pre-race predictor of race outcome in Formula 1, and including it ensures the model has access to the most fundamental competitive signal available. `constructor_tier` provides a coarser but more stable signal about the team's overall capability, helping the model distinguish between contexts where a top-ten finish is nearly guaranteed and contexts where it is genuinely contested. The strategy inputs `n_stops` and `compound_sequence` are what allow the what-if comparison to produce different predictions for different strategic choices. Without them, both scenarios in the comparison would be identical to the model and would return identical probability estimates.

Using the same feature set for both targets is a structural requirement for interpretable what-if disagreement. If the two models used different features, a DISAGREE result — where the two targets recommend opposite strategies — could be explained by the difference in features rather than by the difference in what the two targets measure. By holding the feature set constant, any disagreement between the two models is necessarily a consequence of how the two targets relate to the same predictor variables, not an artifact of model architecture differences.

The model family is Logistic Regression, trained with `class_weight="balanced"` and `max_iter=2000`. The `class_weight="balanced"` setting is essential for the `is_top3` target. With a positive-class rate of 0.155, a model trained without class reweighting would achieve low training loss by predicting the majority class (non-podium) almost always — an approach that is useless for a strategy advisor that needs to discriminate between podium-viable and non-podium-viable scenarios. With balanced class weights, the model assigns per-sample weights inversely proportional to class frequency, so that each podium occurrence in the training data carries approximately 3.2 times the weight of a non-podium occurrence. This makes the model disproportionately sensitive to features that correlate with podium finishes, which is both the reason the model can produce useful what-if signals on `is_top3` and the mechanical root cause of the what-if DISAGREE described in §6.

For `is_top10`, with its near-balanced positive-class rate of 0.517, the balanced weighting has almost no effect — the class weights are nearly equal and the model behaves essentially as an unweighted classifier. This difference in effective upweighting between the two targets is what causes them to weigh the same strategy features differently and to recommend opposite strategies in certain scenarios.

Preprocessing is handled by a `ColumnTransformer` pipeline. Numeric features — `grid_position`, `driver_prior3_avg_finish`, `constructor_prior3_avg_finish`, `driver_circuit_prior_avg`, and `n_stops` — are imputed using the column median, then scaled using `StandardScaler` to zero mean and unit variance. Categorical features — `constructor_tier` and `compound_sequence` — are imputed using the column mode, then one-hot encoded using `OneHotEncoder` with unknown-category handling enabled. The preprocessing pipeline is fit exclusively on the training data and applied to the calibration and test sets, preventing any information from later time periods from influencing the feature representation.

Both models are calibrated using Isotonic regression on the 2022 calibration set. Calibration is necessary because raw Logistic Regression probabilities, particularly on imbalanced targets, tend to be overconfident — they push predictions toward the extremes of zero and one more than the true base rates justify. Isotonic calibration fits a piecewise constant monotone function that maps raw model scores to empirical probabilities observed on the calibration set, improving the correspondence between stated probability and observed frequency. The calibration is implemented using `CalibratedClassifierCV` from scikit-learn, with the base classifier wrapped in `FrozenEstimator` (available from scikit-learn 1.6). The `FrozenEstimator` wrapper prevents the calibration fitting step from refitting the base classifier on the calibration data, which would corrupt the temporal split by allowing 2022 data to influence the parameters of a model that was trained only on 2019–2021 data.

Two baselines are used for comparison. For `is_top10`, the docent reference baseline is provided by the course instructors and reports Brier 0.1320 and ROC-AUC 0.8920 on the same test set. This represents an externally validated benchmark against which the team's model is compared. For `is_top3`, the baseline is a grid-position-only Logistic Regression using the identical pipeline architecture — same preprocessing, same calibration procedure, same temporal splits — but with `n_stops`, `compound_sequence`, and all context features except `grid_position` removed. This baseline was chosen because grid position is the single most informative pre-race predictor of podium finishes available without real-time telemetry. Any model claiming to improve on raw grid position by incorporating additional features must demonstrably outperform this baseline.

For the primary target `is_top10`, the CORRECTED_FEATURES model beats the grid-only baseline on Brier by 0.0189 (0.1374 vs 0.1564), confirming that driver form, constructor tier, circuit history, and strategy inputs add genuine predictive value beyond starting position alone. The ROC-AUC of 0.8805 represents solid discrimination — the model ranks drivers who finish in the top ten above those who do not in 88.05% of pairwise comparisons. However, the model does not reach the docent reference of Brier 0.1320 and ROC-AUC 0.8920; it falls short by 0.0054 Brier and 0.0115 ROC-AUC. This gap is consistent with the team's leaner, more parsimonious feature set of seven features, which prioritizes interpretability and what-if operability over raw predictive power. The model discriminates well but is not state-of-the-art.

For the expansion target `is_top3`, the results are mixed and require honest acknowledgment. The Brier score of 0.0805 is worse than the grid-only baseline of 0.0741 by 0.0064. This is a directionally unfavorable result: the seven-feature model adds complexity — additional features, strategy variables, calibration overhead — without improving probability calibration on podium prediction. The ROC-AUC of 0.9084 marginally exceeds the grid-only baseline of 0.9065, indicating the model ranks podium finishers above non-podium finishers slightly more reliably than the simpler baseline. But ranking and calibration are different properties, and the Brier score captures calibration quality. The lower Brier of the grid-only model means that a model knowing only grid position produces better-calibrated podium probability estimates than a model incorporating form, strategy, and circuit history. The most likely explanation is that podium outcomes are so strongly dominated by car pace — which grid position already encodes — that the additional features add collinearity and calibration noise without contributing predictive signal specific to strategy effects. As a direct consequence, the `is_top3` what-if probability outputs in §6 should be interpreted as directional bounds rather than precise probability forecasts. The model identifies which strategy is more associated with podium finishes under the training distribution, but the gap between 25.0% and 2.1% should not be presented as a precise actuarial estimate.

## §6 — Error Analysis & What-If

**Slice analysis**

The test set was sliced by three dimensions: number of pit stops, circuit type, and constructor tier. The most important findings are summarized below.

On 2-stop strategies (368 test rows), the model achieves a Brier score of 0.1256 for `is_top10` and 0.0809 for `is_top3`, with ROC-AUC of 0.9011 and 0.9284 respectively — the strongest performance of any strategy slice on both targets. By contrast, on 1-stop strategies (353 test rows), the Brier rises to 0.1473 for `is_top10` and 0.0861 for `is_top3`, with ROC-AUC dropping to 0.8605 and 0.9002. This pattern suggests the model is better calibrated and more discriminating for the strategy type that was more common in the training data (2019–2021), when 2-stop strategies were prevalent among competitive cars. On 3+-stop strategies (153 test rows), ROC-AUC falls to 0.8638 for `is_top10`, consistent with the rarity of this strategy type in the training distribution.

On semi-street circuits (118 test rows), the model shows its largest intra-circuit divergence between the two targets: `is_top10` Brier is 0.1727 with ROC-AUC 0.8272 — the weakest circuit-type slice for both metrics — while `is_top3` ROC-AUC reaches 0.9486, the strongest of any circuit-type slice. This split pattern suggests that semi-street race outcomes are highly variable in the mid-pack (making top-ten finishes harder to predict) but more deterministic at the front (where only front-tier cars can realistically reach the podium on a semi-permanent layout). The small sample size of 118 test rows warrants a confidence-interval caveat on the `is_top3` ROC-AUC figure. On permanent circuits (579 test rows) and street circuits (192 test rows), both targets perform comparably to their overall test-set averages, with `is_top10` ROC-AUC of 0.8888 and 0.8855 respectively.

On constructor tier, the pattern is most consequential. For front-tier constructors (170 test rows), whose drivers finish in the top ten 87.6% of the time and on the podium 45.3% of the time, the `is_top10` ROC-AUC drops to 0.6878 — near random — while the Brier of 0.1071 appears favorable only because predicting near-certainty for a class with 87.6% prevalence is accurate on average, not because the model meaningfully discriminates between strategic scenarios. For midfield constructors (407 test rows), both targets achieve their best performance — `is_top10` ROC-AUC 0.8344, `is_top3` ROC-AUC 0.8876 — reflecting the balanced class distributions where strategy choices have genuine discriminative power. For backmarker constructors (312 test rows), the `is_top3` ROC-AUC falls to 0.4952, effectively random, because only 0.6% of backmarker driver-races end in a podium — two positive instances out of 312 — providing no signal for discrimination.

**Three failure-mode hypotheses**

The first failure mode is front-tier is_top10 discrimination collapse. This occurs specifically for Mercedes, Red Bull, and Ferrari-tier constructors on the is_top10 target, where ROC-AUC reaches only 0.6878. The mechanism is straightforward: when 87.6% of a constructor tier's driver-races end in a top-ten finish, there is almost no discriminative signal left for strategy variables to capture. The model can predict that a front-tier car will almost certainly score points, but it cannot reliably identify which strategic choices within that tier produce the best top-ten probability. This failure mode can be tested by restricting training data to front-tier races only and checking whether a tier-specific model recovers meaningful ROC-AUC above 0.75 — if it does, global pooling is the problem; if it does not, the signal genuinely does not exist for this tier and target combination.

The second failure mode is backmarker is_top3 uninformativeness. For backmarker constructors the `is_top3` ROC-AUC is 0.4952, which is below chance. With only two positive podium instances out of 312 test rows, any metric computed on this slice is statistically unreliable. The model achieves a Brier of 0.0064 only because it correctly assigns near-zero probability to almost all backmarker drivers — a trivially correct prediction that requires no strategy discrimination at all. This failure mode cannot be resolved by feature engineering alone; it reflects the real-world fact that backmarker cars almost never reach the podium. Testing it requires either a much larger dataset spanning more seasons, or a fundamentally different approach such as survival analysis or anomaly detection for the rare backmarker podium cases.

The third failure mode is the Brier inversion for is_top3 relative to the grid-only baseline, which is the central calibration problem documented throughout this report. This failure occurs across the full test set, not within a specific slice: the seven-feature model returns Brier 0.0805 against a grid-only baseline of 0.0741. The mechanism is that adding `n_stops` and `compound_sequence` introduces strategy-correlated noise into the probability estimates for a target that is primarily determined by car pace. When `class_weight="balanced"` amplifies the minority-class signal, the model becomes sensitive to pace-strategy co-occurrence patterns in the training data rather than to strategy effects holding pace constant. The test for whether this failure mode is correctable is to retrain the is_top3 model with `class_weight=None` and isotonic calibration on a held-out 2022 set, and check whether the Brier improves below 0.0741 — if it does, the balanced weighting is the specific source of calibration degradation; if it does not, the features themselves are insufficient.

**What-if: Pérez Hungary 2023 DISAGREE**

The what-if scenario holds the following context fixed: Sergio Pérez driving for Red Bull at the Hungarian Grand Prix in the 2023 season, starting from grid position 9. Red Bull is a front-tier constructor with a prior three-race average finish of approximately 5. Pérez has a prior three-race average finish of approximately 7. The Hungaroring is a permanent circuit. Only the strategy inputs are varied.

Under the model and the 2019–2021 training distribution, holding the driver-race context fixed and varying only `n_stops` and `compound_sequence`, the predicted probabilities for both scenarios and both targets are:

| Target | 1-stop M-S | 2-stop M-H-H | Preferred | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| is_top10 | 0.8000 | 0.9136 | 2-stop M-H-H | +11.36 pp |
| is_top3 | 0.2500 | 0.0213 | 1-stop M-S | +22.87 pp |

The verdict is DISAGREE: the two targets recommend opposite strategies. The is_top10 model predicts that the two-stop M-H-H plan raises the top-ten probability from 0.8000 to 0.9136. The is_top3 model predicts that the same two-stop plan reduces the podium probability from 0.2500 to 0.0213.

This DISAGREE is not noise. It is a structural consequence of the asymmetric class distributions between the two targets and the effect of `class_weight="balanced"` operating differently under each distribution. The `is_top10` target has a near-balanced positive-class rate of 0.517, so balanced weighting has almost no effect on the model's sensitivity to any particular feature pattern. The `is_top3` target has a strongly imbalanced positive-class rate of 0.155, so balanced weighting upweights minority-class samples by approximately 3.2 times, making the `is_top3` model disproportionately sensitive to features that co-occur with podium finishes in the training data. At Hungaroring in the 2019–2021 training window, conservative one-stop strategies co-occur with front-tier podium finishes at a higher rate than two-stop strategies, because dominant teams in that era routinely preserved their tyres through one-stop races at this circuit. The `is_top3` model, with its amplified minority-class sensitivity, learns this correlation strongly and predicts a near-zero podium probability for the two-stop scenario. The `is_top10` model does not need this amplification and instead weighs the undercut and overcut opportunities that two-stop strategies create, arriving at a higher top-ten probability for the aggressive plan.

If only the `is_top10` model had been consulted, it would have returned a clear recommendation for the two-stop M-H-H strategy, showing 0.9136 vs 0.8000 — a compelling 11.36 percentage-point gain. There would have been no signal visible in the output indicating that the same strategy collapses the podium probability by 22.87 percentage points. A team whose race objective was to fight for the podium — which is realistic for Red Bull at Hungaroring from ninth — would have followed the recommendation and effectively conceded the podium without knowing it had done so. The dual-target design makes this cost visible, but it does not make the decision. The model presents the trade-off; the strategy engineer states the race objective and chooses accordingly.

The calibration caveat applies to the absolute podium probability values. Because the `is_top3` model does not beat the grid-only baseline on Brier (0.0805 vs 0.0741), the values of 0.2500 and 0.0213 should be read as directional indicators — one-stop is better for podium, two-stop is substantially worse — rather than as precise probability estimates. The direction of the disagreement is robust. The magnitudes are pace-informed bounds, not strategy-isolated forecasts.

## §7 — Limitations and Risks

This model should not be trusted when: (1) the `is_top3` Brier on a subgroup exceeds the grid-only baseline by more than 0.005, indicating the model adds no strategy signal beyond car pace; (2) the race involves a circuit or season not represented in the 2019–2021 training window, such as a new street circuit or a post-2022 regulation change; (3) a safety car or weather event during the race changes the optimal pit window, since the model conditions only on pre-race observables.

The most fundamental limitation is strategy confounding. The model is trained on observational data in which strategy choice is not random and is not independent of car pace, driver skill, weather conditions, or team philosophy. Teams that chose a one-stop conservative strategy at Hungaroring in 2019–2021 may have done so precisely because they had the pace to defend track position through a long first stint — not because the one-stop strategy itself is strategically superior. The model cannot distinguish between these two explanations. When it assigns a high podium probability to the one-stop scenario for Pérez at Hungary, it is reporting a correlation observed in historical data between that strategy choice and podium outcomes for cars in that pace tier and context. It is not reporting the causal effect of choosing one stop over two, holding everything else equal. This distinction matters because a strategy desk acting on the model's output is making a prospective choice, not observing a historical correlation.

The training window (2019–2021) reflects a specific competitive regime — the Mercedes and Red Bull dual-dominance era under pre-2022 aerodynamic regulations — that may not transfer cleanly to the 2023 and 2024 test seasons, let alone to future deployment. The 2022 regulatory change substantially shuffled the competitive order; teams that were classified as midfield in 2019–2021 may have moved to front-tier performance by 2023, while formerly dominant teams may have temporarily regressed. The `constructor_tier` and rolling form features capture some of this shift, but they lag behind real-time performance assessments that a race engineer would apply from practice session data. Consequence: strategy recommendations for constructors that changed tier around the 2022 regulation reset should be treated with additional skepticism.

The scenario surface is deliberately narrow. Only `n_stops` and `compound_sequence` are varied in the what-if comparison; all other feature values — including stint lengths, pit timing, and grid position — are held fixed at the values recorded in the actual historical race row. This restriction preserves leakage integrity but means the model cannot evaluate the effect of changing the pit window lap, the undercut timing, or the response to an emerging safety car. A strategy that would score highly on the model's static evaluation might perform differently in a race where the actual strategic execution diverges from historical patterns at that circuit. The what-if surface is a first-order approximation of the strategic choice space, not a simulation of race dynamics.

## §8 — Reproducibility Note & AI Reflection

The full pipeline is reproducible from a fresh clone of the GitHub repository at https://github.com/IgnaciaHerrera/Capstone.git by following the README runbook. `RANDOM_SEED = 414` is set in every `random_state=` argument throughout the codebase. All dependencies are pinned in `requirements.txt`. Running `hito2_modeling.ipynb` end-to-end from a clean kernel produces all figures, metrics tables, and validation probe outputs cited in this report; no intermediate manual steps are required. The PROMPTS.md file in the Hito 2 folder documents all AI assistance used during the project, including model design decisions, error analysis code, and the writing phase.

Artificial intelligence assistance was used throughout this project, and its use is documented honestly here. Claude Code was used to structure the analysis notebooks, identify the what-if disagreement scenario, generate the leakage guard cell and its column classification logic, and draft prose for the markdown documents including this report. In several instances the AI's output required substantive correction by the team. The most significant correction involved the Step 3b conclusion in `hito2_modeling.ipynb`, where the AI-generated code initially printed a hardcoded conclusion stating that CORRECTED_FEATURES outperformed the grid-only baseline on both targets. This was factually incorrect for `is_top3`, where the computed Brier delta was −0.0064, indicating the model performed worse than baseline. The team caught this discrepancy by reading the computed numbers directly and replaced the hardcoded conclusion with dynamic logic that branches on the sign of the actual delta. All numerical results in this report were validated by the team against the notebook outputs independently; no number was accepted solely on the basis of AI-generated text. The leakage guard column classifications, the interpretation of the DISAGREE scenario, and the calibration caveat connecting the Brier underperformance to the what-if probability values were all developed and verified by the team, with AI serving as a drafting and structuring assistant rather than a subject-matter authority.
